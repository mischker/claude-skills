# Paid Ads — Turn Money Into Leads at Scale

## Overview

Paid ads are 1-to-many advertising to cold audiences. You pay a platform to put your offer in front of their audience. The goal isn't creativity — it's **efficiency**: make more from customers than you spend getting them.

**Key insight:** Ads always work. The only question is *how well* they work. If the right people never see your ad, no ad will work. Start by finding the right people.

---

## The 4 Steps

### Step 1: Pick a Platform

A good platform for you:
1. You've used it and understand how it works as a consumer
2. You can target people interested in your stuff
3. You know how to format ads for it
4. You have minimum budget to place an ad

Start with one platform you understand. Watch the ads on that platform as a user — reverse-engineer what the callouts, value elements, and CTAs look like.

**Platform quick match:**

| Platform | Best for |
|---|---|
| Meta (FB/IG) | B2C, broad consumer, local |
| Google Search | High-intent searchers |
| YouTube | Education, courses, B2B at scale |
| LinkedIn | B2B, high-ticket professional services |
| TikTok | Young B2C, viral consumer products |

### Step 2: Target Your Audience

The right message to the wrong audience = zero results. Your only targeting goal: maximize the percentage of people who might buy your stuff that see your ad.

**Two targeting tools:**

**Lookalike audiences:** Upload your customer list (min 1,000 contacts). The platform finds people statistically similar to them. Priority order for your upload: past customers → warm reach-out list → cold leads.

**Filters on top:** Layer demographic/interest filters to narrow further.
- Only women 25–45? Exclude men.
- Only people with pets? Add pets as interest.
- Local business? Add the city or region.

The more filters → higher efficiency → faster list burn. Start specific (high efficiency, small audience), scale to broader once profitable.

### Step 3: Build the Ad

Every ad has 3 parts: **Callout → Value → CTA**

---

#### Part 1: Callout — Get Them to Notice

The first 3–5 seconds determine everything. "After writing your headline, you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar." — Ogilvy

Your goal: trigger the **cocktail party effect** — even in noise, your name cuts through. Make the right people think "this is for me."

**Verbal callouts:**
- **Labels:** "Clark County Moms," "Agency Owners," "Remote Workers" — group membership triggers attention
- **Yes-Questions:** "Do you wake up to pee more than once a night?" — qualify themselves by answering yes
- **If-Then:** "If you run over $100K/month in ads, we can save you 20% or more…"
- **Ridiculous results:** "This gym owner booked out 2 years in advance — clients furious"

**Non-verbal callouts:**
- Contrast: bright colors, movement
- Likeness: spokesperson looks like your customer ("quack like a duck")
- Scene: show the problem visually (tossing and turning = sleep issues; a cluttered room = hoarding)
- Local landmark: "I know that place!" = instant attention

---

#### Part 2: Value — Get Them Interested

Use the **What-Who-When Framework**:

**What (8 elements of value):**
| Value driver | Good | Bad (to avoid) |
|---|---|---|
| Dream outcome | Show the best possible result | — |
| Opposite nightmare | Show worst case without you | — |
| Perceived likelihood | Past failures explained, success stories like them, guarantees | Risk of failure |
| Speed | How fast they see results | Time delay |
| Ease | What they don't have to do/give up | Effort and sacrifice |
| Opposite hard | Show how hard it is without you | — |

**Who (status perspectives):**
Humans are status-driven. Show how buying your product improves how others treat your customer:
- Spouse: "Your husband won't question this purchase — there's zero risk."
- Kids: "Your kids will notice you have more energy."
- Colleagues: "Your competitors will wonder why their phone stopped ringing."
- Rivals: "Your friend who always has a comment will go quiet."

**When (timeline):**
Show past → present → future consequences of buying AND not buying:
- Past: "You've probably tried [old solution] before and it didn't stick…"
- Present: "Right now you're still [struggling problem]…"
- Future: "In 6 months, you could be [dream result] instead of [nightmare result]."

**Note on length:** Longer ads use more What-Who-When angles. Shorter ads use fewer. Both work — add angles when you have time, remove them when you don't. Keep callouts and CTAs consistent.

---

#### Part 3: CTA — Tell Them What to Do

After value creates motivation, capture it immediately. Motivation is huge but fleeting.

- Be specific: "Click this button" / "Call this number" / "Reply YES" / "Go to acquisition.com/leads"
- Make it easy: short URLs, obvious buttons, simple forms
- Add urgency/scarcity: "This offer closes Friday" / "I can only take 5 this month"

**Landing page rule:** Match the ad's look and language on the landing page. Continuous experience from "click to close." Inconsistency = dropped leads.

---

### Step 4: Get Their Contact Information

After they take action, get permission to contact them. Simple landing page with:
- Headline that matches the ad
- One-sentence explanation
- Form (name + email, or phone)

**Cialdini consistency principle:** Remind them of the action they just took: "Now that you just clicked to learn how to get more gym leads, enter your info and I'll send you the case study."

## The Economics

### LTGP:CAC Ratio

**LTGP** = Lifetime Gross Profit (all revenue from a customer minus cost to deliver)

**CAC** = Cost to Acquire a Customer

**Target:** LTGP:CAC ≥ 3:1

**Benchmark:** Every struggling business Hormozi invested in had LTGP:CAC < 3:1. Getting it above 3:1 was the turning point.

**Diagnosing problems:**
- CAC within 3x industry average → focus on LTGP (improve your offer/upsells)
- CAC > 3x industry average → focus on advertising efficiency (better targeting, better callout, better offer)

**Key insight:** Costs approach zero but revenue can go to infinity. Once your CAC is low enough, the lever is making more per customer — not further cutting acquisition costs.

---

### 3 Phases of Ad Spending

**Phase 1 — Track Money:** Set up tracking before spending a dollar. If you don't track, you'll lose everything. Set up pixel, conversion tracking, UTMs.

**Phase 2 — Lose Money:** Budget = 2x the 30-day cash collected per customer per test. If you make $100 profit in 30 days → spend up to $200 testing each ad. If an ad gets zero leads before spending 1x (e.g., $100), shut it off. Give winners time.

Out of 10 ads: 9 will lose $100, 1 might make $500. Lose $900, make $500 → down $400. But now you have a winner. 100x it: spend $10,000 → make $50,000. The math works over time.

**Phase 3 — Print Money:** Reverse your budget from your sales goal:
- "How many customers do I want this month?" → e.g., 100
- Cost per customer → e.g., $100
- Budget: $10,000 + 20% buffer for efficiency losses at scale = $12,000/month = $400/day

---

### Client-Financed Acquisition

The problem: LTGP is large but most comes later. First 30 days might not cover CAC.

**Solution:** Sell an upsell immediately after purchase that covers CAC in the first 30 days.

**Example:**
- $15/month membership, $5 to deliver = $10 gross profit/month
- Customer stays 10 months = $100 LTGP
- CAC = $30 → 3.3:1 LTGP:CAC ratio (good)
- BUT: $10 in month 1 doesn't cover $30 CAC → cash flow problem

**Fix:** Add a $100 upsell at 100% margin that 1 in 5 customers take = $20 average.
- Month 1 cash collected: $10 + $20 = $30
- This covers CAC → **free customers from month 2 onwards**
- Now you can recycle cash to get more customers without running out of money

This is client-financed acquisition — and it's how you scale without outside funding.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Judging ads too quickly | Budget 2x 30-day cash per test; give each ad enough time |
| Confusing sales problems with ad problems | If leads have the problem and money but don't buy → sales problem, not ad problem |
| Spending on ads before tracking is set up | Set up tracking first — you'll fly blind without it |
| Optimizing creative before testing callout | Callout is the first 5 seconds — test it first and most |
| Scaling before 30-day profitability | Fix client-financed acquisition before increasing spend |
| Best content not repurposed | Top-performing free content often makes the best paid ads |
