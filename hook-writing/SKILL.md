# Hook Writing — The First 5 Seconds

## Overview

"After you've written your headline, you've spent eighty cents of your advertising dollar." — David Ogilvy

A hook is whatever gets attention first — the first 3–5 seconds of a video, the first image your eyes land on, the first line of an email. Everything else is irrelevant if the hook fails.

**The cocktail party effect:** Even in a noisy room, your name cuts through everything. A good hook does that — it makes the right person think "this is for me" in a crowd of content.

**ROI of good hooks:** Mastering hooks can 5x, 10x, or 100x the number of people who pay attention to your ads, content, and outreach — without changing anything else.

---

## How Hooks Work

Every hook has two parts:

1. **Callout** — makes the right person say "this is for me" (triggers the cocktail party effect)
2. **Value condition** — implies or states: "if you consume this, you will get [benefit]"

Together: *If [you, the target person] [do this thing], you will get [this value].*

## Verbal Hook Types

### Labels
Words the avatar identifies with. Triggers group membership recognition.
> "Local business owners, I have a gift for you"
> "Agency owners…"
> "Burnt-out founders…"

### Yes-Questions
Questions where your avatar answers yes — qualifying themselves.
> "Would you pay $1,000 to have the business of your dreams in 30 days?"
> "Do you wake up to pee more than once a night?"

### Conditionals
Scenarios or conditions leading to a result or command.
> "If you're working all the time and your business isn't growing, you're working on the wrong sh*t."
> "If you run over $100K/month in ads, we can save you 20%…"

### Commands
Direct instructions to a specific person.
> "Read this if you're tired of being broke."
> "Stop doing X before you lose your business."

### Statements
Bold claims or curiosity triggers.
> "The smartest thing you can do today…"
> "How to get ahead of 99% of people."

### Lists / Steps
> "The 28 ways to stay poor."
> "3 things I did to go from $0 to $100M."

### Narratives
Story openers that create immediate curiosity.
> "One day I was in the back and this old lady comes in piss angry."
> "I had a rough 90 days. My grandfather died…"

### Exclamations / Provocative Statements
Strong emotion or surprise.
> "Ahhhh… this is the blueprint to becoming a millionaire and I'm going to walk you through the levels."

## Non-Verbal Hooks (for video/image)

- **Contrast:** Bright colors, fast movement, visual pattern breaks
- **Likeness:** Spokesperson who looks exactly like your customer ("quack like a duck")
- **Scene:** Show the problem visually — person tossing in bed (sleep problem), cluttered house (hoarding), empty gym (growth problem)
- **Local landmark:** "I know that place!" — instant connection, stops scroll

## The 70-20-10 System

**70% — Proven winners.** Use hooks that already worked in your own content and ads. Stabilizes performance, guarantees baseline results.

*Action: Create a spreadsheet with tabs per platform. Track: hook text, views/results, link. Review your top performers before every recording session.*

**20% — Winner-adjacent.** Model hooks that work in adjacent niches or on other platforms. If a hook structure crushes on YouTube, adapt it for Meta ads.

*Action: Subscribe to tools that surface content outliers. Search top-performing posts in your topic area. Collect hooks in a swipe file.*

**10% — New experiments.** Totally new formats, angles, or creative approaches. High risk, potential for breakthrough. If it wins, it becomes part of the 70%. If it loses, document it so you don't repeat it.

---

## Hook Production Protocol

**Spend 80% of your prep time on hooks. 20% on the body (meat). Almost nothing on CTAs.**

For ads specifically:
- Record 10 hooks per 1 ad content piece
- Test them against each other, keep the winner, kill the rest
- Same body. Same CTA. 10 different hooks. One winner.

For content:
- Every top-performing tweet or caption on any platform → potential hook for another platform or ad
- Cross-pollinate aggressively

**Building your hook library:**
1. Best hooks from your own past content (winners only)
2. Best hooks from your own paid ads (profitable ones)
3. Hooks from other people's high-performing content in your niche
4. Hooks from other people's successful ads
5. Platform ad libraries (last resort — hard to know what's actually converting)

## Hook Templates by Awareness Level

For ads that need to reach beyond your warm audience:

| Awareness Level | Hook Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Most aware | Offer-driven | "50% off [product] for the rest of the month" |
| Product-aware | Proof-driven | "Why 10,000 people chose [X] last month" |
| Solution-aware | Promise-driven | "The fastest way to [result] — introducing [X]" |
| Problem-aware | Pain-driven | "Tired of [specific problem]? There's a better way" |
| Completely unaware | Curiosity-driven | "The hidden danger in your daily routine that's costing you [X]" |

As your ad spend scales, you need hooks for each awareness level to convert the broader market.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Making the hook about you | It should make them feel important, not you |
| Using vague callouts ("entrepreneurs") | Get specific: "Agency owners billing under $50K/month…" |
| Spending equal time on hook and body | Hook gets 80% of prep time. It decides everything |
| Not building a hook library | Track every winner. Never start from scratch again |
| Testing only one hook per piece of content | Record 10 hooks per ad. Most will lose. One will crush |
