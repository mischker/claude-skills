# Referral System — Turn Customers Into Lead Getters

## Overview

Referrals are exponential, not linear. Every other advertising method scales linearly (2x effort = 2x leads). Referrals compound — one customer brings two, who bring four, who bring eight.

**Why most businesses don't get referrals:**
1. Their product isn't as good as they think it is
2. They don't ask for referrals

**The formula:**
> Price is what you charge. Value is what they get. The difference is **goodwill**. Goodwill creates referrals.

**The referral growth equation:**
- Referrals > churn → business grows without any other advertising
- Referrals = churn → you need ads to grow
- Referrals < churn → you need ads just to stay flat (most businesses)

---

## Part 1: Build Goodwill (Make the Product Referable)

If you sell direct to consumers and they're not sending friends, your product has room to improve.

Ask yourself: **"Why are my customers too embarrassed to tell everyone they know about my product?"**

### 6 Ways to Build More Goodwill

**1. Sell better customers (Call Outs)**

Figure out what your most successful customers have in common. Change your targeting to attract more of them. Better-fit customers get more value → more goodwill → more referrals. Also: better-fit customers lower your advertising costs (more specific messaging = more efficient ads).

*Process:* Look at your lowest-churning or highest-satisfaction customers. What niche, situation, or problem type do they share? Target only that going forward.

**2. Set better expectations (Dream Outcome)**

Under-promise, over-deliver. Most businesses promise the moon to close sales, then barely meet expectations.

Start inching down your promises until close rates drop. Stop there. This maximizes goodwill because every customer is surprised by how much they got. Customers don't compare what they got to what they paid — they compare it to what they expected.

**3. Get more people better results (Perceived Likelihood)**

Find out what your best customers did differently. Then make every new customer do those things.

*Hormozi's process:*
1. Survey customers → find who got the best results
2. Interview them → what did they do differently?
3. Find common actions
4. Force new customers to do those actions
5. Measure improvement in average customer results
6. Build guarantees around those actions (getting them to do what creates success)

*Gym Launch example:* Gym owners who ran paid ads and made a sale in the first 7 days had 3x the LTGP. So Gym Launch focused everyone's onboarding on hitting that milestone.

**4. Give faster wins (Time Delay)**

Break outcomes into the smallest possible increment. Communicate more frequently. Force a good first impression in the first 48 hours.

*Tactics:*
- Deliver 7 small wins instead of 1 big win — same total value, 7x the emotional wins
- Add progress updates even when there's no new progress
- Set timelines with 50% buffer so you always deliver early
- BAMFAM: Book A Meeting From A Meeting — never leave customers in no-man's land
- Customers form lasting impressions in the first 48 hours — stack wins in that window

**5. Keep making the product better (Effort & Sacrifice)**

The less customers have to do/give up to benefit, the more goodwill you get.

*Monthly process:*
1. Gather: customer service data, surveys, reviews → find most common problem
2. Research: interview customers who made it work despite the problem → get their workaround
3. Fix: use that feedback to improve
4. Test: roll out to small group of struggling customers
5. Measure: did it solve the problem? If yes, roll out to all. If not, iterate.
6. Repeat: move to next most common problem

**6. Give them something to buy next (Call to Action)**

Customers who bought your first offer want more. If you don't sell them something next, they'll buy from someone else. 

Keep selling. Customers who've bought from you refer their friends to subsequent purchases too — sometimes even when they didn't refer on the first one.

## Part 2: Ask for Referrals

> Asking for referrals only works when you treat it like an offer — showing what the customer gets when they refer.

**The meta-question:** Before asking, ask yourself: "Why would referring be good for this customer?" Then sell that benefit.

### 7 Ways to Ask

**1. One-sided referral benefit**
Pay the referrer up to your CAC when their friend signs up. Or give the friend a discount equal to your CAC.

> "I can write you a check for $200 when your friend signs up. Want to make that intro now?"

Spouse referrals work especially well — give a household discount (they both benefit).

**2. Two-sided referral benefit (Dropbox/PayPal model)**
Split your CAC between referrer and friend (50/50). Both win.

> "For every friend you send us, you get $100 and they get $100 off."

**3. Ask at the point of purchase**
On the sales contract or checkout page: "Who else would you like to do this with?"

Script: "People who do our program with someone else get 3x the results — who else could you do this with?"

Result: One salesman at a portfolio company shattered sales records doing only this. Half his sales were referrals from a single question.

**4. Referrals as a negotiation chip**
If someone wants a discount, trade it for an introduction.

> "I can't do anything less than $500, but if you make a 3-way text intro to a few friends right now, I'll waive the initiation fee."

If a full-price customer finds out: "I gave them $100 off for 3 referrals — happy to do the same for you. Who do you have in mind?"

**5. Referral events (time-limited promotion)**
Create a 1–4 week campaign where customers get credits, cash, or status for bringing friends.

Names that work:
- "Bring-a-Friend Promotion"
- "Spouse Challenge"
- "Accountability Buddy Promotion"
- "Coach Challenge" (team-based with employees and customers)

**6. Ongoing referral program**
Talk about the benefits of doing things with others in all content, outreach, and onboarding — all the time.

A friend running virtual events saw a 33% boost in signups after implementing an ongoing program. 250,000 of 1,000,000 customers were referred.

**7. Unlockable referral bonuses**
Create status-based bonuses unlocked by referrals:
- VIP status
- Premium support
- Additional service hours
- Courses, training, merchandise

Unlockable bonuses cost less than cash but can be made highly desirable.

### The Gift Card Play
Give customers a gift card for 1/3 of the program cost. They give it to a friend as a "gift" — not a referral. It creates social capital for the referrer ("I got you $2000 off"). Add an expiration date (7–14 days) to force use. Text a picture with the friend's name on it.

## Referral Program Structure

Three components: **incentive type + what you give + how you ask.**

| Incentive type | When to use |
|---|---|
| Cash | Maximum activation (best referrers) |
| Store credit | High perceived value, lower cost |
| Discount | Good for friend; motivates referrer via status |
| Status/unlockables | Low cost, high perceived value |
| Gift cards | Creates social capital for referrer |

**Referral tiers:** Don't cap payouts. The best programs incentivize endless referrals with increasing rewards for higher-volume referrers.

## Benchmarks

- Referrals covering >5% of new customers monthly → your product is starting to compound
- Referrals covering >25% of new customers → strong word of mouth; double down on goodwill
- Referrals > churn → you can stop worrying about advertising for growth

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| "We just need to get the word out" | If your product were exceptional, word would be out already |
| Never asking for referrals | Most customers will refer if you simply ask — they just need a prompt |
| Asking without showing what they get | Treat referral asks as offers — show the value of referring |
| One-off referral campaigns | Build referral asks into every purchase and win moment |
| Ignoring the 48-hour window | First impression is permanent — stack wins immediately after purchase |
