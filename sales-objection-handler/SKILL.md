# Sales Objection Handler — Hormozi Scripts

## Overview

Objections are not rejections. They are requests for more information or signals of a specific fear. Your job: find the real objection, address it, and ask again.

**Core rule:** Never lower the price for the same product. Address the fear behind the objection, then offer a different structure (payment plan, feature change) if needed.

**Context:** These scripts slot into Phase 5 of a sales call. See `sales-call-structure` for the full call flow.

---

## The Objection Framework

**Every objection has a real one behind it.**

1. Acknowledge (don't argue)
2. Clarify (find the real objection)
3. Address (resolve the specific fear)
4. Ask again (close)

---

## "It's Too Expensive"

**Almost never means price. Usually means: "I don't see enough value yet" or "I can't afford it upfront."**

**Script:**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — is it that [price] is more than you have available right now, or is it that you're not sure it's worth [price] for you specifically?"

- If **affordability** → go to Payment Plan Downsell (`mm-payment-plan-downsell`)
- If **value doubt** → re-anchor value:
  > "What would it be worth to you if [specific outcome they said they wanted]? ... So at $[price], you're paying $[X] to get $[Y value]. Does that feel off?"

Then close again.

---

## "I Need to Think About It"

**Almost always means: "I'm not convinced" or "I'm afraid of a mistake."**

**Script:**
> "Totally fair. What specifically are you thinking through? Is it the [price], the [timing], whether it'll work for you, or something else?"

Get them to name the real thing. Then address only that.

If they can't name anything specific:
> "In my experience, when people say they need to think about it, it usually means something isn't sitting right — and I'd rather know what that is so I can either address it or tell you honestly it's not the right fit. What's the hesitation?"

## "I Need to Talk to My Spouse / Partner"

**Real objection: Either they genuinely need approval, or they're using it as an escape.**

**First, qualify:**
> "Absolutely — would your [spouse] have any concerns I could help you address right now so you're prepared for that conversation?"

If they can't name any concerns → they may be the real decision-maker:
> "What would you tell them about why you're interested in this?"

Get them to sell themselves. Then:
> "And what do you think they'd push back on?"

Address the anticipated objection. Then offer a callback when spouse is available.

**If they genuinely need partner approval:**
> "Is there a time in the next 24–48 hours when we could do a quick call with both of you? I want to make sure all your questions get answered."

## "I've Been Burned Before"

**Real objection: Likelihood doubt. They don't believe this will work.**

**Script:**
> "That makes complete sense — and honestly, I'd be suspicious of anyone who'd never been burned before. Can you tell me what happened?"

Let them vent fully. Then:
> "What would have made that situation different? What would have needed to be true for it to work?"

Map their answer to your offer's specific features. Then:
> "Does [X feature of your offer] address that? ... So the reason [previous thing] failed was [reason]. In our case, [specific reason that's different]. Does that shift anything?"

---

## "I Don't Have Time Right Now"

**Real objection: They don't believe the ROI on their time is worth it.**

**Script:**
> "That's fair. How much time were you expecting this to take? ... What if it took [X hours/week]? Would that be workable?"

If they say yes → lock in a start date and structure around that time.

If still no:
> "What's the cost of not solving [problem] for another [timeframe]? If we solved it in 30 days, what would that be worth?"

ROI reframe: low time investment vs. high cost of inaction.

---

## "Let Me Shop Around First"

**Script:**
> "Absolutely — you should. What are you going to be comparing? Price? Results? Support? ... What would make [competitor] or [alternative] better than what we've talked about?"

Get their criteria. Then show how you match/beat each one.

If they still want to shop:
> "Here's what I'll do — I'll keep [this offer / this price / this slot] available for 48 hours while you look. After that I can't guarantee the same terms. Does that work?"

Deadline creates real urgency without false scarcity.

---

## "Now's Not a Good Time"

**Script:**
> "I get it. When would be a better time? ... What needs to happen between now and then for it to make sense?"

Get a concrete future trigger. Then:
> "And what would change between now and [then] that would make [problem] easier to solve?"

If nothing will change → the timing isn't the issue:
> "It sounds like the timing may not change much — would it make sense to at least get started on [small first step] so [problem] isn't still sitting there in [X months]?"

---

## "I Can Get It Cheaper Elsewhere"

**Script:**
> "You probably can. What's different about the cheaper option?"

Let them list the differences. Then highlight the value of what they'd be giving up:
> "If [feature they'd lose] is important to you — and it sounds like it is — then the cheaper option might not actually solve the problem. Would you rather pay less and not get [outcome], or pay [your price] and get it?"

## General Objection Principles

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| Acknowledge before countering | "That makes sense" / "I hear you" first |
| Ask before answering | Find the real objection; don't guess |
| Never argue | "You might be right" opens doors; arguing closes them |
| Close after addressing | Always re-ask after resolving an objection |
| Three nos = offer something different | After three genuine nos, pivot offer structure |
