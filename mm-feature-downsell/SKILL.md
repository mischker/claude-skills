# Feature Downsell — Downsell Offer

## Overview

When a customer says no to the full offer AND payment plans don't work: remove a feature, lower the price, ask "how about now?" Repeat until they buy something.

**Core mechanic:** You're not discounting — you're personalizing. Lower price is justified by less product. The customer sees the value of what you removed only after you remove it, which often gets them to re-upsell themselves on the original offer.

**Key rule:** Never lower the price for the same product. Change what they get, then change the price.

## What to Remove

**Quantity downsell**
> "Instead of four sessions a month, why don't we start you at two?"
> "Instead of a 3-month supply, let's start with one month."

**Quality downsell**
> "Instead of leather seats, we can do vinyl."
> "Instead of 5-minute response time, let's do overnight — still get answers, just with a delay."

**Quality dimensions for services:**
- Response speed (minutes vs. hours vs. days)
- Availability (24/7 vs. 9-5, all locations vs. one)
- Delivery format (live vs. recorded, in-person vs. remote)
- Service ratio (1:1 vs. group)
- Provider seniority (owner vs. employee)
- Personalization (custom vs. generic)
- Guarantee terms (unconditional vs. conditional)
- Support method (video call vs. chat vs. email)

**Remove an entire feature**
> "If you don't need the money-back guarantee, I can knock $400 off."

Removing a guarantee often backfires — customers realize its value and buy the original. That's the point.

**DFY → DWY → DIY downsell**
> "If we can't work together on this, I'd still love to help. Here's a DIY course that walks you through the same process."

## The Naming Framework

Name packages after status / aspiration:
- Most expensive: "The Whale," "VIP," "Platinum," "Total Transformation"
- Middle: standard tier name
- Cheapest: **"The Minimum"** (implies: at least get this much)

> "So, nothing more than the Minimum package then?" → gets them to say no to saying no

## Process Flow

1. Remove highest-value feature, lower price a lot → many re-upsell themselves on the original
2. Remove next feature, lower price a bit more → gets the budget-constrained buyer
3. After 2 feature downsells, temperature check: 1–10 scale
4. If 8+: switch to Payment Plan Downsell → combine both
5. If 7 or below: find what a 10 looks like → recombine features for that

After each step: **"Deal?" or "Fair enough?"** — this simple close works.

## Free Orientation Technique

When everything fails:
> "Even though we're not going to work together on X, I'd love to help. Can you come to a free orientation on [topic] tomorrow?"

At the end: sell a DIY product that solves the same problem. ~50% show up; nearly all buy.

## Barter for Reviews/Testimonials

Discount in exchange for advertising:
> "I'll knock $100 off if you: leave reviews on all sites, record a video testimonial, make 3 social posts during the program, and introduce me to two friends. Deal?"

The advertising value exceeds the $100 discount every time.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Lowering price without removing features | Always justify lower price with less product |
| Removing what they love → they walk | Remove secondary features first; keep core value |
| Removing what they hate → they don't re-upsell | Remove high-value features to trigger reconsideration |
| Giving up after 2–3 downsells | Alternate between feature and payment plan downsells |
| No "The Minimum" package | Always have a baseline that's easy to say yes to |
