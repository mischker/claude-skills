# Menu Upsell — Upsell Offer

## Overview

Tell customers what they don't need (unsell), then tell them exactly what they do need and how to use it (prescribe), then let them pick their preference (A/B), then make paying easy (card on file).

**Core mechanic:** Remove the "do I buy this?" question by removing options they don't need, and turning every remaining choice into "which version?" not "yes or no."

## The Four Tactics

### 1. Unsell
Cross out what they don't need based on their situation.

> "You're not trying to gain weight, right? OK — you don't need this protein gainer. And you're not pre/post-surgery, so we can skip the lymphatic stuff. That leaves us with these three."

Crossing things out builds trust and goodwill. Use crossed-out items to highlight what remains.

### 2. Prescribe
Tell them what they need and exactly how to use it — as if they've already bought it.

> "You're going to take two of these in the morning, one of these after your workout, and this one at night. That combination handles [their problem] in about 3 weeks."

Don't ask if they want it. Tell them how they'll use it.

### 3. A/B Offer
Never ask "do you want this?" — ask "which one?"

> "Do you prefer chocolate or vanilla?" / "Morning or evening appointments?" / "Monthly or quarterly?"

Turn every decision into a preference question. Either answer = a sale.

**Make anything A/B-able:** quantity (1 bottle or 2?), start date (Monday or Tuesday?), format (read or listen?), flavor, time slot, delivery speed, personnel.

**Add a nudge:** "I like the chocolate — that's what most people get." Nudge toward the item you want to move.

### 4. Card on File
Instead of asking them to pay, reference what's already there.

> "Do you want to just use the card we have on file?"

If first purchase: "What card do you want to use?" (Not "do you want to pay?")

## The Economist Play (Two Products, Want Both)

**When to use:** You have two complementary products (A and B) and want most customers to buy both, but they default to choosing one.

**When NOT to use:** If Option B is significantly better than the bundle on its own, or your margins don't support bundling at the same price.

If you have Option A ($59), Option B ($125), and want people to buy both:
1. Add Option C (A+B) at same price as B ($125)
2. People compare B and C → C is obviously better value at the same price
3. Most buy C (both)

**Why it works:** People compare visually adjacent options. B and C sit at the same price — C is clearly more. B exists only to make C feel like a deal. Without B, people compare A vs. C and often choose A.

## Full Script Template

> "Let me walk you through what you actually need. [Cross out 2–3 irrelevant items.] Given your situation, you're going to need [item 1] — you'll take that [specific instruction]. And [item 2] — [specific instruction]. Now, [item 1] comes in [A] or [B] — I like [A] but [B] is great too. Which do you prefer? Great. Do you want to just use the card on file?"

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Showing everything → overwhelm | Unsell first; limit visible options to 3 or fewer |
| Asking "do you want to buy?" | Only ask "which one?" — A/B everything |
| Vague instructions → doubt | Prescribe exactly how to use it; make it personal |
| Asking for card explicitly | "Card on file?" or "What card?" — not "will you pay?" |
