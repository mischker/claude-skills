# Price Raise — How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers

## Overview

Most business owners fear raising prices more than they fear going out of business. But charging too little creates a vicious cycle, while charging more creates a virtuous one.

**The virtuous price cycle:**
Higher price → clients invest more → perceived value increases → results improve → clients demand less → more service is possible → more results → more profit → reinvest in ads and product → higher price justified

**The vicious price cycle (most businesses):**
Lower price → less client investment → lower perceived value → worse results → more demanding clients → less money to serve them → less conviction in sales → lower close rate → lower price required

"There is no strategic advantage to being the second-lowest price in the marketplace. But there is for being the highest." — Dan Kennedy

---

## Rules Before Raising Prices

**Don't grandfather existing customers.** If you lock in a price, you lock in your ceiling. Never promise a "lifetime price."

**Never sell "one-time price for lifetime access"** unless delivery costs exactly $0 forever. Every company that tries this ends up in trouble when the money runs out but the obligation doesn't.

**Test on new customers first.** Before raising prices on your existing base, test the new price on incoming customers. This gives you conversion data and confidence before the bigger announcement.

**Raise prices at least once per year.** Warren Buffett raised See's Candies prices ~10%/year for 50+ years. He made it his sole decision-making domain. If it was worth his time, it's worth yours.

**Pair with a launch when possible.** The best time to raise prices is within 90 days of releasing new features, products, or improvements. Frame as early-adopter pricing.

**Do the math first.** Know what percentage of customers you can lose and still make more money. Usually it's higher than you think.

**Meet with people individually if raising >50%.** For large increases, 1-on-1 conversations before the announcement dramatically reduce loss.

## How to Pick the New Price

Test: take your current conversion rate × current gross profit. Then model what happens at higher prices where conversion drops.

```
$10 → 5% conversion × $100 LTV = $500 revenue per 100 leads
$20 → 4% conversion × $200 LTV = $800 revenue per 100 leads  ← winner
$100 → 2% conversion × $300 LTV = $600 revenue per 100 leads
```

The best price is the one that maximizes (conversion rate × LTV) — not the one that closes the most deals.

**Pro tip:** Start low, raise 20% every 10 sales until conversion drops materially. Back off 10% from that point. You've found the sweet spot.

## The RAISE Letter Framework

When raising prices on existing customers, use this structure:

### R — Remind them of the value you've already delivered
Open by listing specific results, improvements, or wins the customer has gotten since working with you. Make it tangible. This anchors the value in their mind before you mention money.

### A — Address the price change directly
Don't bury it or soften it excessively. State the new price clearly and when it takes effect. Customers respect directness more than vague corporate language.

### I — Invest in their future
Explain what the new revenue enables: better product, more support, additional resources. Make the price raise feel like a shared investment in the outcome they care about.

### S — Soften with a loyalty reward
Existing customers who stay get something new customers won't: grandfathered-in bonuses, early access, extended payment terms, or a special thank-you gift. Make loyalty feel valuable.

### E — Explain away their concerns
Address the most common objections proactively:
- "What if it doesn't work?" — reinstate your guarantee or success process
- "Will I get more for the price?" — list specific improvements
- "What if I can't afford it?" — offer payment plan option

## Template Sequence

1. **Write the letter** using RAISE framework
2. **Record a video** reading the letter (more personal than email alone)
3. **Post the video** to your customer community with comments off (redirect questions to DMs or direct contact)
4. **Send email + text** linking to the video
5. **Follow up 1-on-1** with anyone who responds negatively

For >50% increases: personal call or video call with each customer before announcing publicly.

## What to Expect

You will lose some customers. This is normal and acceptable. The question is whether the math works:

**Break-even analysis:**
```
Current: 100 customers × $100 = $10,000/month
New price: $200 (100% increase)
Break-even: need only 50 customers to stay → same revenue
If 70 stay: $14,000/month (+40%)
If 80 stay: $16,000/month (+60%)
```

In practice, less than 10% of customers cancel when raises are communicated well and paired with demonstrated value increases.

Trey's gym example: tripled monthly membership prices. Business went from losing $2,000/month to profiting $4,000/month — in one week.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Waiting until the business is failing | Raise at least annually, regardless of whether you need to |
| Apologizing for the raise | State it directly. Apologetic tone signals the raise is unjustified |
| Grandfathering existing customers | Sets a ceiling on your pricing power forever |
| Raising without showing new value | Pair raises with product improvements or capability expansions |
| Not testing on new customers first | Test new price on incoming customers before existing base announcement |
