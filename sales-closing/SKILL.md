# Sales Closing — Give People the Power to Decide

## Overview

**Most salespeople confuse "no" and "I'm avoiding a decision."** They look the same — neither results in a purchase. But they're completely different. People who won't buy are done. People avoiding a decision just need more power to decide.

**Closing is not manipulation.** It's giving someone enough information and clarity to make a genuine decision. You should be equally OK with a yes or a no — both are better than a stall.

**The three buckets advertising creates:**
1. **YESes (laydowns)** — They'll buy regardless; advertising closed them. Just don't mess up the sale.
2. **NOs (unqualified)** — Wrong fit. Move on quickly.
3. **MAYBEs (on the fence)** — They value your offer but have reservations. This is where all the money is. This is who you train for.

The better your advertising, the more maybes you get. Closing determines who converts from that group.

---

## The Onion of Blame

People avoid purchasing decisions by blaming something external. The blame is rarely the real issue — it's a rationalization for avoiding commitment. Peel back each layer until you reach the real concern.

**Three layers of blame:**

### 1. Circumstances
"Buying this conflicts with something I want more, or prevents me from doing/buying something I'd rather do."

Common forms:
- **Time:** "I don't have time for this right now" / "I'm too busy"
- **Money:** "This is too expensive" / "I can't afford it"
- **Fit:** "I need something different" / "Not right for me"

### 2. Other People
"Someone else doesn't trust me to make this decision, or I don't trust you enough to buy."

Common forms:
- **Authority/Permission:** "I need to check with my husband/partner/boss before deciding"
- **Trust/Skepticism:** "I've been burned before" / "Let me think about it" / "What makes you different?"

### 3. Self
"I might not succeed, or I'm scared to repeat past failures."

Common forms:
- **Fear:** "It might work for others, but it won't work for me" / "I'm not sure I can commit"
- **Avoidance:** "I need to think about it" / "Send me more information"

**Key insight:** People often present 3–4 layers of blame. When you address one and they raise another, it doesn't mean you failed — it means you're closing correctly. Keep peeling.

## Rules of Closing

**1. Repeat back what they said.** Buys you time to think. Demonstrates listening. Neutralizes hostility.

**2. Acknowledge, never disagree.** The moment you argue, they dig in. Instead:
- "That's a great point, thank you for bringing that up…"
- "I totally understand…"
- "You're completely right, I should have mentioned…"

Then reframe or offer a different perspective — but not a counter-argument.

**3. Get permission before getting direct.** Before delivering a hard truth:
- "Can I be a coach instead of a friend for a second?"
- "Would it be totally overstepping for me to put my coach hat on?"
- "Can I tell you what I told someone in your exact position just yesterday that helped them make this decision?"

**4. Stack closes.** One close rarely works alone. If the first doesn't land, try a different angle or analogy. Each layer brings them closer.

**5. You don't need to memorize — you need to understand.** Once you understand why blame robs people of decision-making power, you can respond in your own words. Understanding the logic beats memorizing scripts.

## Closes by Blame Type

### When They Blame Circumstances — Time

> "I totally understand, and I'm curious — if the time concern went away, is this something you'd move forward with? [Wait] I ask because in my experience, when someone says they don't have time, it usually means one of two things: either the outcome isn't compelling enough, or they're genuinely stretched thin. Which is it for you?"

If genuinely busy:
> "That's actually why our clients get started — they tell me later they wished they'd started sooner, because the problem doesn't go away while you're busy. It compounds."

### When They Blame Circumstances — Money

> "I understand. Let me ask — is it that you don't have the money, or that you're not sure it's worth the money? Because those are really different problems. [Wait] If you knew for certain it would work, would the money be an obstacle?"

Then address the certainty concern (guarantee, case study, success mechanism).

### When They Blame Other People — Need Approval

> "I totally get that. Quick question — in your experience, is [partner/boss] usually supportive of decisions like this that could help [outcome they want]? [Wait] And if they weren't sure, what would be their main concern? Let me address that now so you can have that conversation confidently."

Get the decision-maker on the call when possible.

### When They Blame Other People — Past Bad Experience

> "I hear you. That's really common and honestly, if I were you, I'd be cautious too. What specifically went wrong? [Listen] The reason I ask is because what you described is exactly what our [mechanism / approach / guarantee] is designed to prevent. Here's how we're different…"

### When They Blame Self — Fear It Won't Work for Them

> "Can I be real with you for a second? [Wait for permission] The people we've seen get the best results are exactly the ones who feel the way you're feeling right now — skeptical, cautious, once-burned. The people who fail are the ones who think it's going to be easy. You're thinking about this seriously, which means you're the kind of person who makes it work."

### When They Stall — "I Need to Think About It"

> "Of course. What specifically do you need to think about? [Listen] Because I want to make sure you have all the information to make a good decision. Usually when someone says that, it's [time/money/doubt it'll work/permission]. Which is it?"

Get the real objection. "Think about it" is never the real objection.

## The Post-Close

After they say yes:
1. Confirm payment and start date immediately
2. Resell the decision: "Great choice. Here's exactly what happens next…"
3. Deliver a quick win in the first 48 hours (sets retention expectation)

After they say no (genuine no):
1. Ask: "Just so I can improve — what would have made this a yes for you?" (Valuable market research)
2. "Would it be okay if I reached out in 90 days? Sometimes timing changes."
3. End warmly. Genuine nos turn into yeses months later when circumstances change.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Accepting "no" when it's really "avoiding a decision" | Ask "is this a no forever, or just not right now?" |
| Arguing with objections | Acknowledge first, then reframe |
| Treating all objections as price objections | Find the actual blame layer first |
| Stopping at the first objection | Expect 3–4 layers. Keep peeling |
| Making the close about you making a sale | Closing gives them the power to decide. You benefit either way |
